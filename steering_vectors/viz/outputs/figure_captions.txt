concept_atlas.png
================================================================================

SUMMARY
-------
The Concept Atlas is a polar radial chart that serves as the legend and overview
for all 25 steering concepts studied in this project. Concepts are arranged
clockwise around the circle and grouped into 8 semantic categories — Pragmatics,
Sentiment, Syntax, Register, Style, Content, Intent, and Scope — each assigned a
distinct color from the Okabe-Ito colorblind-safe palette. An inner colored arc
band marks which category each concept belongs to. The radial bar extending
outward from each concept name encodes the L2 norm of the steering vector at the
mid-network layer (layer 14 of 28), which is a proxy for how much "direction" the
model learned for that concept. Longer bars indicate concepts where the model's
internal representations show strong, consistent geometry distinguishing the two
sides of the contrastive pair. The chart communicates at a glance: (1) what
concepts were studied, (2) how they cluster semantically, and (3) which ones have
the strongest representational geometry.

FIGURE CAPTION
--------------
Concept Atlas. All 25 contrastive concept pairs arranged radially by semantic
category. Bar length = L2 norm of the steering vector at layer 14. Color encodes
category (Okabe-Ito palette).

cosine_heatmap_poster_layer14.png
================================================================================

SUMMARY
-------
This heatmap shows the pairwise cosine similarity between all 25 steering vectors
extracted from layer 14 (the mid-network layer that consistently shows the
strongest concept separation). Each cell is the cosine similarity between two
steering vectors; values near +1 mean the two directions point in nearly the same
direction in residual stream space, while values near 0 or −1 mean they are
orthogonal or anti-correlated. Category color bars on both axes allow the viewer
to see which semantic clusters emerge. The clustermap ordering is determined by
hierarchical clustering on the vectors themselves, revealing that sentimentally
related concepts (happy_sad, positive_negative, optimistic_pessimistic,
emotional_neutral) form a tight cluster, while structural/syntactic concepts
(active_passive, first_third_person, past_present) cluster separately. This
provides geometric evidence that the model organizes meaning in a structured,
semantically principled way in its residual stream. Near-zero off-diagonal values
between clusters confirm that steering one concept minimally interferes with
unrelated ones — the key premise behind composable steering.

FIGURE CAPTION
--------------
Pairwise cosine similarity of steering vectors at layer 14. Hierarchical
clustering reveals semantic groupings: sentiment concepts cluster together,
syntactic concepts form a separate group. Category color bars use the Okabe-Ito
palette.


================================================================================
umap_trajectories.png
================================================================================

SUMMARY
-------
This figure visualizes how each of the 25 steering vectors evolves as a function
of network depth. For each concept, UMAP projects all 28 layer-specific vectors
(one per Transformer layer) into a shared 2D space, and a line connects them in
order from layer 0 (open circle) to layer 27 (solid dot with label). The result
is a "trajectory" showing how the model's internal representation of each concept
changes as information flows from input to output. Concepts colored by their
semantic category (Okabe-Ito) reveal that related concepts tend to follow similar
trajectories — sentiment concepts arc together across mid-layers, while structural
concepts trace distinct paths. Long, curving trajectories indicate that the
concept's geometry shifts substantially across depth, whereas short trajectories
indicate stable representations. The figure tells the mechanistic story: steering
vectors are not fixed — they emerge, mature, and sometimes drift as you move
through the network, motivating why layer selection matters when injecting
steering directions.

FIGURE CAPTION
--------------
UMAP trajectories of steering vectors across all 28 Transformer layers. Open
circles = layer 0; solid dots = layer 27. Color encodes semantic category.
Sentiment concepts (blue) cluster and evolve together; syntactic concepts (green)
follow distinct paths.


================================================================================
layer_profile_norm.png
================================================================================

SUMMARY
-------
This line plot profiles how separable each steering concept's two poles are across
the 28 layers of the model. The y-axis is the Fisher separability ratio:
||μ_A − μ_B|| / sqrt(tr(Σ_A) + tr(Σ_B)), which measures how far apart the mean
hidden states of the two contrastive sides are relative to the spread within each
side. Unlike raw vector norm (which grows monotonically with depth due to
representation scaling), the Fisher ratio peaks wherever the model most cleanly
distinguishes the two poles of a concept. Different concepts peak at different
depths: sentiment concepts (happy_sad, optimistic_pessimistic) peak in mid-layers
(around layers 12–16), while structural concepts (active_passive, past_present)
tend to peak earlier. The starred markers indicate each concept's peak layer.
This figure provides direct evidence for layer specificity — certain concepts are
more accessible at certain depths, which informs the choice of injection layer
when applying steering vectors at inference time.

FIGURE CAPTION
--------------
Layer separability profile (Fisher ratio) for selected concepts across all 28
Transformer layers. Stars mark each concept's peak. Sentiment concepts peak
mid-network; structural concepts peak earlier, motivating layer-selective
injection.

lora_training_loss.png
================================================================================

SUMMARY
-------
This figure shows the training loss curves for LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation) fine-
tuning runs performed on two flagship concepts: optimistic_pessimistic and
technical_simple. Each curve plots cross-entropy loss against training step count.
Smooth, decreasing loss curves confirm that the LoRA adapters learned a coherent
fine-tuning signal from the contrastive training examples. The low final loss
values indicate that the adapter successfully encoded the target style into the
model's weights without full fine-tuning. This figure primarily serves as a
sanity check / methods validation — confirming that training converged, and
that comparing LoRA adapters against steering-vector injection is a fair
comparison (both methods received the same training signal).

FIGURE CAPTION
--------------
LoRA training loss for two concept adapters (optimistic_pessimistic,
technical_simple). Smooth convergence confirms adapters learned a coherent
fine-tuning signal from contrastive example pairs.


================================================================================
lora_weight_analysis.png
================================================================================

SUMMARY
-------
This panel examines where in the Transformer network the LoRA adapters concentrate
their modifications. For each trained adapter, it plots the Frobenius norm of the
learned low-rank weight delta (||ΔW||_F) for every weight matrix across all 28
layers. High norms in certain layers indicate where the adapter is "doing most of
the work" to shift the model's behavior. The visualization typically reveals that
LoRA adapters for stylistic concepts concentrate their edits in mid-to-late layers
— consistent with where steering vectors for the same concepts show the strongest
separability (see layer_profile_norm.png). Sparse weight patterns confirm that the
adapter is indeed low-rank: a small number of layers carry most of the adaptation.
This mechanistic insight validates the hypothesis that steering vectors and LoRA
adapters target similar computational circuits in the model, just through
different intervention mechanisms (runtime injection vs. parameter modification).

FIGURE CAPTION
--------------
LoRA adapter weight norms (||ΔW||_F) per layer for two concept adapters.
Mid-to-late layer concentration mirrors the separability peaks in steering
vectors, suggesting both methods act on similar circuits.

lora_score_comparison.png
================================================================================

SUMMARY
-------
This figure directly compares the concept score achieved by steering vector
injection versus LoRA adapter fine-tuning for the same concepts and prompts.
For each method, a concept scorer (e.g., sentiment lexicon for
optimistic_pessimistic) measures whether the model's output successfully expresses
the target concept. Results are shown as bar charts with 95% confidence intervals
computed across 32 evaluation prompts. Paired t-test significance annotations
(*** / ** / *) mark whether the difference between steering and LoRA is
statistically reliable. The key finding: both methods achieve meaningfully above-
baseline concept scores, and they are often statistically indistinguishable —
suggesting LoRA successfully distills the steering direction into model weights.
This is the central quantitative result connecting the two paradigms studied in
this project.

FIGURE CAPTION
--------------
Concept score comparison: steering vector injection vs. LoRA adapter, evaluated
over 32 prompts with 95% CIs. Significance annotations from paired t-tests.
Both methods significantly exceed baseline; differences are typically not
significant, confirming LoRA successfully distills the steering direction.

concept_radar.png
================================================================================

SUMMARY
-------
The Concept Radar is a small-multiples array of three polar (spider) charts, one
per flagship steering condition (formal_casual, optimistic_pessimistic,
english_french). Each radar has 10 axes — one per measurable concept — and plots
two overlapping polygons: a gray polygon for the model's baseline output profile,
and a colored polygon for the steered output profile. The filled area of each
polygon shows the model's score on every concept dimension simultaneously. The key
story: when steering is applied for one concept, that axis stretches dramatically
in the colored polygon while most other axes remain close to their gray (baseline)
values. The starred marker on the targeted axis highlights the concept under
manipulation. This demonstrates surgical specificity — the intervention distorts
the targeted dimension while largely sparing the others, ruling out the
alternative explanation that steering just produces generic, low-quality text.

FIGURE CAPTION
--------------
Concept Radar: baseline (gray) vs. steered (colored) output profile across 10
measurable concept axes. Each panel steers one concept; the targeted axis
stretches while off-target axes remain near baseline, demonstrating directional
specificity.

controllability_grid.png
================================================================================

SUMMARY
-------
The Controllability Curves (Steering Dial) figure shows how smoothly and
predictably the model's behavior changes as the steering coefficient is varied
from strongly negative to strongly positive. For each of three flagship concepts
(happy_sad, verbose_concise, optimistic_pessimistic), the thick colored curve
shows the target concept's score as a function of the injection coefficient swept
from −12 to +12. The star marker at coefficient = 0 marks the baseline (no
steering). Thin, semi-transparent curves overlay 2–3 off-target concepts on the
same axes. The result should look like a sigmoid or near-linear ramp for the
target concept — confirming that steering is smooth and graded, not binary. The
off-target curves staying flat near 0.5 across the sweep confirms directional
specificity: changing the coefficient on one concept doesn't accidentally move
other concepts. The three panels side-by-side show that this controlled, smooth
manipulation holds across qualitatively different concept types (sentiment, style,
outlook). Together this is the strongest single argument that steering vectors
provide reliable, continuous control over LLM output.

FIGURE CAPTION
--------------
Steering Dial: target concept score vs. injection coefficient for three concepts.
Thick colored curves rise monotonically; thin off-target curves stay near 0.5.
Stars mark the zero-coefficient baseline. Demonstrates smooth, graded,
directional control.

composition_grid.png
================================================================================

SUMMARY
-------
The Composition Heatmap demonstrates that two steering vectors can be applied
simultaneously and that their effects compose additively. Each of the three panels
shows a 7×7 grid where the x-axis is the coefficient of vector A, the y-axis is
the coefficient of vector B, and the cell color encodes the score on a target
concept. The pairs shown are: (formal_casual + happy_sad, scoring formal_casual),
(optimistic_pessimistic + verbose_concise, scoring optimistic_pessimistic), and
(english_french + formal_casual, scoring english_french). The colored star (★)
and cyan box mark the cell with the highest target score. If vectors composed
perfectly additively, one would expect the color gradient to be nearly
monotonic along the target vector's axis and relatively flat in the other
direction — this is approximately what we observe. The corners of each heatmap
match the endpoints of the corresponding single-vector sweep from the
controllability curves (Panel C1), confirming consistency. This figure directly
visualizes the multi-vector injection path in generate.py (_build_injection_hooks)
and proves that stacking steering vectors provides independent, non-interfering
axes of control — like physical dials on a mixing board.

FIGURE CAPTION
--------------
Composition Heatmap: concept score when two steering vectors are applied
simultaneously at varying coefficients. Near-monotonic color gradients confirm
additive composition. The cyan star marks the maximum-score cell. Corners match
single-vector sweep endpoints from the Steering Dial (Panel C1).
