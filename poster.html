Steering Vector Analysis: Mechanistic Interpretability of Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct

Noah Bates  ·  Richard Zhang  ·  Aneya Sobalkar  ·  Adithya Giri  ·  Safin Singh
Machine Learning @ Berkeley  ·  April 30, 2026
Abstract

We build a mechanistic interpretability pipeline for Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct, computing steering vectors for 25 contrastive concept pairs across all 28 transformer layers.

  • Steering vectors: L2-normalized mean-difference activations → (25 × 28 × 1536) direction tensor.
  • Affective concepts co-cluster by layer 21 (cosine similarity ≥ 0.75); English/French is a persistent outlier throughout.
  • Steering provides directional behavioral control — noisy at high magnitudes, with crosstalk on off-target axes.
  • Rank-16 LoRA adapters converge smoothly but fail to shift concept scores; weight deltas remain near-orthogonal to steering vectors at every layer.
Introduction

Mechanistic interpretability aims to reverse-engineer LLM behavior by analyzing the geometry of internal activations. Steering vectors — residual stream directions computed as mean-differences across contrastive prompt pairs — exploit the finding (Turner et al., 2023) that high-level semantic concepts are encoded as approximately linear directions in activation space.

We investigate how 25 concept directions relate geometrically across all 28 transformer layers of Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct, and whether this geometry can be permanently encoded into weights via LoRA fine-tuning — moving interpretability from observation toward persistent, parameter-level behavioral control.

Background

High-level semantic concepts can be approximately localized as linear directions in a transformer's residual stream. Steering vectors exploit this: computed by contrasting the model's internal representations of two opposing behavioral modes, then injected at inference time to shift behavior toward one pole.

Computing a steering vector for concept C:

  1. Craft contrastive prompt pairs eliciting each pole (e.g., optimistic vs. pessimistic)
  2. Extract residual stream activations at every layer via PyTorch forward hooks
  3. Compute the L2-normalized mean-difference: v = (μA − μB) / ‖μA − μB‖
  4. At inference time, inject v × α into the residual stream; α controls direction and magnitude
Methodology

We systematically extract and analyze steering vectors for 25 contrastive concept pairs spanning 8 semantic categories, covering a wide range of linguistic and behavioral dimensions:

  • Pragmatics: formal/casual, polite/rude, urgent/calm, question/statement
  • Sentiment: positive/negative, happy/sad, optimistic/pessimistic, emotional/neutral
  • Syntax: active/passive, past/present, first/third person
  • Register: technical/simple, scientific/colloquial, english/french
  • Style: verbose/concise, long/short, specific/vague, creative/literal
  • Content, Intent, Scope: instructional/narrative, refuse/comply, assertive/hesitant, and more

Batched forward passes produce a (25, 28, 1536) direction tensor. Geometric structure is analyzed via cosine heatmaps and UMAP. Rank-16 LoRA adapters (q_proj + v_proj) are trained on steered outputs and evaluated via concept scoring and cosine alignment between weight deltas and steering vectors.

Results — Semantic Geometry

Concept Directions Cluster by Meaning with Depth

At layer 14, affective concepts (optimistic/pessimistic, happy/sad, positive/negative) form a dense cluster with similarities ≥ 0.75; syntactic concepts form a separate orthogonal group. Near-zero off-diagonal values between clusters confirm low cross-concept interference — a prerequisite for composable behavioral control.

Fig. 2 — Cosine Similarity at Layer 14. Pairwise cosine similarities of all 25 steering vectors. Hierarchical clustering reveals semantic groupings: affective concepts form the dense warm block (top-right), syntactic concepts cluster separately. Category color bars use the Okabe-Ito palette. Near-zero off-diagonal values between clusters confirm low interference.

Vectors Progressively Diverge from a Shared Origin

All 25 concepts originate near a common point at layer 0, then fan out into semantically organized regions by layer 27. English/French is the most isolated concept at every layer, reflecting its fundamentally different linguistic basis. Semantically overlapping pairs (verbose/concise, long/short) maintain near-identical trajectories throughout, confirming shared geometry reflects shared meaning.

Fig. 3 — UMAP Trajectories Across 28 Layers. Each line shows one concept's steering vector as it evolves from layer 0 (open circle) to layer 27 (solid dot). Color encodes semantic category. All concepts originate near a shared early-layer origin and progressively specialize into semantically organized clusters.
Results — Steering Effectiveness

Directional Control — With Noise at High Magnitude

Varying α shifts the target concept score in the expected direction, but the response is noisy rather than monotonic — especially at high |α| where over-steering degrades coherence. verbose_concise shows the clearest signal; happy_sad is noisiest. Off-target scores also fluctuate rather than staying flat.

Fig. 4 — Steering Dial. Target concept score (thick colored line) vs. injection coefficient α for three concepts. Target curves show a general directional trend but with significant noise, especially at high |α| where over-steering degrades coherence. Off-target curves (thin) also fluctuate rather than staying flat. Stars mark the zero-coefficient baseline.

Concept Shift — Substantial Crosstalk at High Coefficient

At α = +20, steering visibly shifts the targeted axis (starred) but also broadly distorts off-target axes. English/French produces the most extreme reorganization — the polygon collapses on most other dimensions while spiking toward the target. Even formal/casual and optimistic/pessimistic show substantial polygon deformation beyond the starred axis, indicating that high-coefficient injection is not surgically specific.

Fig. 5 — Concept Radar (α = +20). Baseline (gray) vs. steered (colored) output profiles across 10 concept axes. The targeted axis (starred) shifts strongly, but at this coefficient off-target axes also distort substantially — confirming that high-magnitude steering is directional but not surgically specific.
Results — Layer Depth & LoRA Distillation

Each Concept Peaks at a Concept-Specific Depth

Concepts show distinct separability profiles across depth. Formal/casual and technical/simple peak early (layers 4–6), reflecting register-level properties near the input. Optimistic/pessimistic and english/french peak later (layers 19–25), requiring deeper integration. This motivates layer-selective injection: injecting at the wrong depth produces weaker or noisier effects.

Fig. 6 — Separability Profile. Normalized Fisher ratio across all 28 transformer layers for 5 representative concepts. Stars mark each concept's peak separability layer. Early-peaking concepts (formal/casual, technical/simple) differ markedly from late-peaking ones (optimistic/pessimistic, english/french).

LoRA Converges but Cannot Encode Steering Geometry

Rank-16 LoRA adapters (q_proj + v_proj) were trained on steered outputs, with the hypothesis that fine-tuning on these examples would encode the steering direction into weights. Training loss converges smoothly, but evaluating the adapters reveals the core failure:

Fig. 7 — LoRA Training Loss. Smooth convergence for both adapter concepts confirms the training signal was coherent and that the adapters learned from the contrastive examples. Loss converges within ~60–70 steps in both cases.

Despite growing weight norms, cosine alignment between LoRA deltas and steering vectors stays near zero at every layer. The adapter modifies weights — but not in the steering direction. It learned to mimic the surface form of steered outputs without capturing the underlying geometry.

Fig. 8 — LoRA Weight Analysis. Left: LoRA weight norm ‖ΔW‖F grows toward later layers, confirming the adapter is making meaningful weight modifications. Right: Cosine alignment between those weight updates and the corresponding steering vectors stays near zero at every layer — the adapter's updates are orthogonal to the steering direction throughout the network.
Key finding: Behavioral steering and LoRA fine-tuning operate through fundamentally different internal mechanisms. Imitating steered outputs teaches the adapter to mimic the surface form of behavior, but does not transfer the directional geometry that makes the behavior work into weight space.

Qualitative Examples — Steering vs. Baseline

Optimistic Steering (coeff +18, layer 27) — "Can we solve climate change?"

Baseline: "Solving climate change is an ambitious and complex task that requires the collective effort of individuals, businesses, and governments worldwide…"

Steered: "Yes, we can work towards solving climate change through various efforts and strategies. 1. Reduce Carbon Emissions… 2. Promote Renewable Energy…"

Assertive Steering (coeff +20, layer 27) — "Should I eat a banana or an apple in the morning?"

Baseline: "It depends on your personal preferences and dietary needs. Both bananas and apples can be healthy options…"

Steered: "Both are healthy, but bananas tend to be higher in potassium and natural sugars for energy. Choose whichever you prefer based on your dietary needs!"

Conclusion
  • Concept directions sharpen into tight semantic clusters by layer 21; specialization is a function of depth, not an input-level property.
  • Affective concepts co-cluster (similarity ≥ 0.75); English/French remains uniquely isolated throughout all 28 layers.
  • Steering provides directional control — noisy at high magnitudes with measurable crosstalk, but not a binary flip.
  • LoRA fails to internalize steering geometry — weight deltas remain near-orthogonal to the target direction despite smooth convergence.
  • Future work: Representation-aware training objectives that directly optimize LoRA delta alignment with steering vectors.
